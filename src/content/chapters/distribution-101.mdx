## Opening Vignette

Picture this: It's 11 p.m. on a Tuesday, and a consultant in São Paulo is scrolling through her phone, searching for a hotel in Midtown Manhattan for next week's client meeting. Within minutes she has compared prices on Booking.com, glanced at Google Hotels, checked the Marriott app for loyalty points, and tapped "Book Now" — all without speaking to a single human being.

Meanwhile, the revenue manager at that Manhattan hotel is watching the reservation appear in real time on their dashboard, one of perhaps 400 bookings flowing in that day through more than a dozen different channels — the hotel's own website, three OTAs, a global distribution system serving corporate travel agents, a wholesale bed bank supplying a Japanese tour operator, and a metasearch click that converted on the brand app. Each booking arrived through a different pathway, at a different cost, carrying different data about who the guest is and what they expect.

That constellation of pathways — and the strategy behind choosing, managing, and optimizing them — is **hotel distribution**.


## Learning Objectives

Upon completing this chapter, you should be able to:

1. **Define hotel distribution** and articulate why it is the commercial backbone of the hospitality industry.
2. **Trace the historical evolution** of hotel distribution from handwritten ledgers to AI-powered platforms, identifying the technological breakthroughs that reshaped the business at each stage.
3. **Identify and describe** the primary direct and indirect distribution channels, the major players in each, and the economics that govern them.
4. **Explain fundamental concepts** — channel mix, cost of distribution, Net RevPAR — and why they matter for profitability.
5. **Connect distribution knowledge** to career paths across hotel operations, revenue management, marketing, ownership, and technology.
6. **Recognize emerging trends** — from generative AI to the wholesale boom — that are reshaping the landscape right now.

It is the connective tissue between a hotel's *supply* and the world's *demand* for accommodation. But calling it "just reservations" would be like calling the internet "just email." ### Why Distribution Is the Engine of Hotel Commerce

A hotel is, at its economic heart, a perishable-inventory business. Every unsold room-night vanishes at midnight — it cannot be warehoused and sold tomorrow. This makes distribution not merely operational but *existential*. Its impact is best understood from three perspectives:

**For hotels**, a well-executed distribution strategy directly drives the ability to:

- **Maximize occupancy** by reaching diverse guest segments across geographies.
- **Optimize revenue** by placing the right room in front of the right guest at the right price, through the most profitable channel — the discipline captured in the metric *Net RevPAR* (Revenue Per Available Room after distribution costs).
- **Build visibility** by maintaining consistent visibility on the platforms where target customers search.
- **Access new markets** through channels with specific regional or demographic reach.

**For guests**, modern distribution systems deliver:

- **Choice and transparency** — the ability to compare thousands of options, prices, and reviews in seconds.
- **Convenience** — seamless booking across mobile, desktop, voice assistants, and messaging apps.
- **Personalization** — increasingly tailored offers driven by loyalty data and browsing behavior.

**For hotel owners**, the single most consequential strategic decision is whether to affiliate with a brand or operate independently. In the U.S., **72% of hotel rooms** are now branded — up from just 46% in 1990 — and the gap is widening. As of September 2024, branded hotel rooms (4.1 million) were growing at 1.1% year-over-year while independent rooms (1.6 million) were declining at −1.7%, pushed out by tight debt markets and rising construction costs that disproportionately impact properties without the financial backing and franchise models of major chains (Agarwal, 2025a; Borko, 2021).

This brand concentration is a defining structural shift: distribution capabilities — loyalty programs, brand.com technology, GDS connectivity, and negotiating leverage with OTAs — are the primary reasons owners choose to affiliate. As Skift Research's analysis of the U.S. ownership landscape concluded, "the most important decision any hotel owner needs to make is whether to affiliate their property with a franchise brand or to distribute and market the hotel independently" (Borko, 2021). Globally, the pattern is different: in Europe, independents still hold the majority at roughly 63% of rooms, underscoring that the brand-dominant model is primarily an American phenomenon (Borko, 2021).

**For the broader travel economy**, the stakes are enormous:

- In 2024, travel and tourism contributed **$10.9 trillion** to global GDP — roughly 10% of the world economy — and supported **357 million jobs**, approximately one in ten worldwide (World Travel & Tourism Council [WTTC], 2025).
- International visitor spending grew **11.6%** year-over-year in 2024, reaching $1.9 trillion, while domestic visitor spending hit $5.3 trillion (WTTC, 2025).
- The global hotel industry generated an estimated **$965 billion** in revenue in 2024 and is expected to surpass **$1 trillion** in 2025 — making it the largest single sector in the travel economy (Zanpure, 2025).
- The U.S. lodging industry alone generated over **$197 billion** in room revenue in 2024 (STR, 2025).

The global online travel market alone reached an estimated **$1.13 trillion** in 2024 and is forecast to grow to **$1.76 trillion** by 2029 at a CAGR of 9.18% (GlobalData, 2025a). The efficiency and reach of hotel distribution systems enable this economic activity. As the HSMAI Foundation noted in its landmark *Distribution Channel Analysis*, understanding how to manage demand alongside the associated costs of distribution is paramount — especially because new channels primarily shift market share rather than create entirely new demand (Estis Green & Lomanno, 2012, p. 11).


## 1.3 The Evolution of Hotel Distribution: A Story in Five Acts

Today's distribution ecosystem did not appear overnight. It is the product of decades of invention, disruption, and reinvention — and the history matters because it reveals the competitive dynamics and path dependencies that still govern every hotel's strategy.

### Act I: The Age of the Ledger (Before the 1950s)

Hospitality is one of humanity's oldest enterprises, rooted in the ancient duty to shelter the traveler. From the caravanserais along the Silk Road to the *xenia* (guest-friendship) of Ancient Greece and the *hospitium* agreements of Rome, providing for the weary journeyer has been a cultural cornerstone across civilizations (O'Gorman, 2010).

For most of this long history, "distribution" was beautifully simple: a sign by the road, a reputation carried by word of mouth, a direct inquiry from a traveler who had arrived in town. The Nishiyama Onsen Keiunkan in Japan, founded in 705 CE and recognized by Guinness World Records as the world's oldest continuously operating hotel, thrived for centuries with nothing more than local renown (Guinness World Records, 2011).

As the modern hotel industry emerged in the 19th and early 20th centuries, innkeepers managed rooms with handwritten ledgers. Reservations arrived — if they arrived at all — by mail, telegram, or telephone. The system was localized, labor-intensive, and prone to the bane of every hotelier's existence: the double booking. It worked, but only because the world moved slowly.

### Act II: The Machine Age — Early Automation (1940s–1960s)

The post-war travel boom changed everything. Commercial aviation was shrinking the globe, interstate highways were connecting American cities, and a new middle class was discovering the joy of the road trip. Hotels needed to handle reservation volumes that handwritten ledgers simply could not manage.

- **1947:** Westin introduced **Hoteltype**, an early teletype-based system enabling inter-property reservation communication — a guest in San Francisco could now book a room in New York through a connected network.
- **1958:** Sheraton launched **Reservatron**, widely regarded as the first automated electronic reservation system for hotels, complete with a toll-free telephone number for direct bookings (Hotel Tech Report, 2020).

These were modest systems by today's standards, but they represented a conceptual leap: for the first time, hotel inventory could be managed *electronically* across multiple locations.

### Act III: The GDS Revolution (1960s–1980s)

The airline industry, facing the staggering complexity of managing millions of seats across global route networks, became the unlikely architect of modern hotel distribution.

**The birth of SABRE.** In the early 1960s, a landmark collaboration between American Airlines and IBM produced the **Semi-Automated Business Research Environment (SABRE)** — a computerized reservation system that digitized passenger records and flight inventories. What had taken hours could now be done in seconds (Hopper, 1990).

Other airlines followed: United built Apollo, TWA created PARS, and European carriers would eventually develop Galileo and Amadeus. These airline CRSs were technological marvels of their era — real-time, transaction-processing systems operating at massive scale.

**From airline tool to global marketplace.** To extend their reach, airlines placed CRS terminals directly in travel agency offices. Initially airline-specific, these terminals became multi-supplier platforms after U.S. airline deregulation in 1978 created fierce competition for travel agency shelf space. The CRSs evolved — often spun off as independent companies — into **Global Distribution Systems (GDSs)**:

- **Sabre** (from American Airlines)
- **Apollo/Galileo** (United Airlines → merged under Travelport)
- **Amadeus** (founded 1987 by Air France, Iberia, Lufthansa, and SAS)
- **Worldspan** (Delta, Northwest, TWA → eventually absorbed by Travelport)

Hotels quickly recognized the opportunity. Being listed on the GDS meant access to the lucrative corporate travel market managed by travel agents worldwide. Hotel GDS bookings grew from **less than 2%** of total volume in the early 1980s to **over 20%** by 1999 (Estis Green & Lomanno, 2012, p. 13).

**The Switch.** Connecting a hotel's Central Reservation System to multiple GDSs — each with proprietary data standards — was expensive and complex. In 1989, a consortium of 16 hotel companies founded **THISCO (The Hotel Industry Switch Company)**, which built a centralized translation layer enabling a single "one-to-many" connection. This dramatically lowered the barrier to electronic distribution for chains and independents alike. THISCO evolved into **Pegasus Solutions**, a major distribution technology provider (Estis Green & Lomanno, 2012, p. 13).

### Act IV: The Internet Disruption (1990s–2000s)

If the GDS era was a revolution, the internet was an earthquake.

**1994:** THISCO launched **TravelWeb**, the first website enabling consumers to search and book hotel rooms in real time — initially featuring Hyatt Hotels. For the first time, a traveler could bypass intermediaries entirely and book directly through a screen in their living room.

**The brand.com race.** Hotel companies moved fast. Hyatt launched its website in 1994, Choice Hotels and Holiday Inn followed in 1995, and Marriott and Hilton were online by 1996. The promise was intoxicating: a direct-to-consumer channel with dramatically lower transaction costs than any intermediary.

**The OTA explosion.** But hotels were not the only ones who saw the internet's potential. In **1996**, two launches reshaped the industry forever:

- **Travelocity** (from Sabre, leveraging its GDS infrastructure)
- **Expedia** (from Microsoft, initially using Worldspan GDS data)

These Online Travel Agencies offered consumers something hotel websites could not easily replicate: the ability to compare multiple hotels side by side, bundled with flights and car rentals, with user reviews adding a layer of social proof. By 2000, more than 20% of all hotel reservations were electronic (GDS and internet combined), with 10–12% of electronic bookings originating from internet sites (Estis Green & Lomanno, 2012, p. 14). Carroll and Siguaw (2003), writing in the *Cornell Hotel and Restaurant Administration Quarterly*, were among the first academics to map this rapidly evolving electronic distribution landscape, documenting how new intermediaries were fundamentally reshaping the balance of power between hotels and distributors.

**Priceline and the opaque model.** Priceline.com introduced "Name Your Own Price" in 1998 — an opaque model where travelers bid on hotel rooms without knowing the brand until after purchase. It was controversial, but it solved a real problem: helping hotels fill distressed inventory without publicly discounting their brand.

**The post-9/11 pivot.** The travel downturn following September 11, 2001, paradoxically accelerated OTA growth. Desperate for occupancy, hotels turned to OTAs at aggressive commission rates and deep discounts. It was a Faustian bargain: the rooms filled, but the OTAs gained enormous market power and consumer loyalty that would prove difficult to claw back.

### Act V: The Modern Era — Metasearch, Mobile, and the Battle for the Guest (2004–Present)

The mid-2000s through today has been defined by an ever-expanding universe of channels and an intensifying battle between hotels and intermediaries for control of the customer relationship.

**Metasearch arrives.** In 2004, **Kayak** and **Skyscanner** launched, introducing the metasearch model: aggregate prices from OTAs and hotel websites, let the consumer compare, then redirect them to the chosen source to complete the booking. Google's entry into this space — first through Google Hotel Finder (2011), then the far more powerful **Google Hotel Ads** — fundamentally altered the competitive landscape. Google's dominance in search meant it could insert itself at the very top of the booking funnel.

**Mobile reshapes everything.** The iPhone's debut in 2007 triggered a tectonic shift. Kayak launched one of the first travel mobile apps in 2009; by the mid-2010s, more than half of travel searches were happening on mobile devices. HotelTonight (founded 2010) pioneered same-day mobile booking, proving that last-minute, phone-first behavior was a permanent shift, not a fad.

**The direct booking wars.** By the mid-2010s, major hotel brands launched aggressive campaigns to reclaim direct bookings. Hilton's landmark **"Stop Clicking Around"** campaign (2016), detailed in Chapter 4, and Marriott's **"It Pays to Book Direct"** initiative offered loyalty members guaranteed best rates and exclusive perks — marking a strategic inflection point in the battle for channel mix.

**The social and conversational era.** The rise of Instagram, TikTok, and influencer culture transformed travel inspiration. Platforms like **Instagram**, **TikTok** (where viral hotel tours drive demand), and **WeChat** (which functions as an all-in-one booking super-app in China) blurred the line between content and commerce. Meanwhile, **generative AI tools** — from ChatGPT-powered trip planners to Google's AI Overviews — are beginning to reshape how travelers *discover* hotels, potentially disintermediating even the search engines that disintermediated the travel agents.

## 1.4 The Distribution Landscape Today: Key Players and Core Concepts

Today's hotel distribution ecosystem is a densely interconnected network. Understanding its players and the economics governing their interactions is essential for any hospitality professional.

### The Channel Map

#### Direct Channels
These are pathways where the hotel owns the customer relationship and the transaction.

- **Hotel Website & Mobile App (Digital Direct):** The hotel's owned digital storefront. Direct digital channels accounted for **20%** of global hotel gross bookings in 2024, up from 17% in 2019, and are projected to become the single largest channel by 2030 (Skift Research, 2024a, p. 4). Consumer surveys corroborate this trajectory: **59% of travelers** report booking flights and accommodations directly with suppliers, and **51%** say they have increased direct bookings since COVID (Skift Research, 2024b).

- **Voice (Central Reservations & Property Direct):** Telephone bookings through a brand's call center or the hotel directly. Despite the digital revolution, voice remains significant — particularly for complex itineraries, group inquiries, and high-value guests who want human reassurance.

- **Walk-In:** Guests arriving without a reservation. Small in volume but high in margin — no acquisition cost whatsoever.

- **Group Sales:** Direct sales to secure meetings, conferences, weddings, and tour group bookings. Group business accounted for **14%** of global gross bookings in 2024 (Skift Research, 2024a, p. 4).

- **Social Commerce:** An emerging channel as platforms like Instagram, TikTok, and WeChat enable booking through social media content. Social commerce combines travel inspiration with instant conversion, and its share of hotel bookings is expected to grow as platforms add native booking functionality (Skift Research, 2025).

Collectively, offline direct channels (voice + walk-in) represented **15%** of global gross bookings in 2024. In 2023, **40%** of U.S. consumers reported booking their hotel stays directly with the hotel company (Skift Research, 2024b, p. 133).

#### Indirect Channels
These involve third-party intermediaries that facilitate the booking, earning commissions, markups, or fees.

- **Online Travel Agencies (OTAs):** Digital marketplaces — Booking.com (Booking Holdings), Expedia.com (Expedia Group), Trip.com Group, and regional players. The OTA market reached **$266 billion** in gross bookings in 2024, compared to **$262 billion** for hotel direct channels — a narrowing gap that signals the intensifying battle for the guest (Schaal, 2024). Four major OTA conglomerates — Booking Holdings, Expedia Group, Airbnb, and Trip.com — control approximately **66%** of global gross bookings (Zanpure, 2025; Agarwal, 2025c). OTAs deliver massive reach and sophisticated marketing engines — but at the highest cost. Commission structures and their impact on hotel profitability are analyzed in depth in Chapter 8.

- **Global Distribution Systems (GDS):** The legacy electronic platforms — Amadeus, Sabre, Travelport — connecting hotels to traditional travel agencies, corporate travel departments, and travel management companies (TMCs). The GDS accounted for **10%** of global bookings in 2024 (Skift Research, 2024a, p. 4). While growth has stalled, the GDS remains indispensable for corporate and managed travel.

- **Wholesalers & Bed Banks:** Entities like Hotelbeds, WebBeds, and W2M that contract room inventory at negotiated net rates and redistribute it to OTAs, tour operators, and retail agents. This channel has surged — from **12%** of global bookings in 2019 to **16%** in 2024 — driven by the resurgence of international leisure travel and the complex, multi-layered distribution chains in Asia-Pacific and Europe (Skift Research, 2024a, p. 4).

- **Metasearch Engines:** Google Hotel Ads, Trivago, Kayak, and Tripadvisor aggregate rates from multiple sources, letting consumers compare and then click through to the booking source. They operate primarily on **cost-per-click (CPC)** or **commission-per-acquisition (CPA)** models. In 2023, 12% of U.S. consumers booked hotels via metasearch (Skift Research, 2024b, p. 133). Google's dominance here — controlling the top of the search funnel — makes it an increasingly powerful gatekeeper.

### Fundamental Concepts

#### Channel Mix

Channel mix is the specific blend of distribution channels a hotel uses to sell its rooms — for example, 30% brand.com, 22% OTAs, 12% GDS, 10% wholesale, 14% group, 12% voice/walk-in. A 2025 study by HEDNA, NYU Tisch Center, and RateGain — surveying 700+ hotel brands across 21,000+ properties — found a remarkably even five-way split: OTAs (20%), walk-ins and group (21%), direct digital/web (19%), direct voice (21%), and GDS (18%) (HEDNA, NYU Tisch Center, & RateGain, 2025). The strategic objective is an **optimal channel mix**: one that maximizes net revenue by balancing occupancy, ADR, and acquisition cost while aligning with the hotel's brand positioning and target segments.

There is no universal "correct" mix. It varies by property type (branded chain vs. independent boutique), location (urban CBD vs. beach resort vs. airport), size, target market (corporate-heavy vs. leisure-dominant), and market conditions. Branded hotels are generally less reliant on OTAs than independents — the brand's loyalty program and marketing machine provide a built-in demand engine that independents must replicate through other means (Skift Research, 2024a, p. 13; broadly consistent with Phocuswright's U.S. Online Travel Overview series and the HOTREC European Hotel Distribution Study described below). The HOTREC European Hotel Distribution Study, conducted biennially by Schegg (2022) at the HES-SO Valais-Wallis in Switzerland, provides the most comprehensive longitudinal data on channel mix evolution across European hotels, tracking how direct and indirect channels have shifted over more than a decade. Because of market fragmentation, European hotels typically rely more heavily on OTAs and other indirect channels than their U.S. counterparts — a pattern documented across multiple waves of the HOTREC study. De Vega-Ventura and Coenders (2024) further advanced this analysis using compositional data methods on real hotel management system data from 2013 to 2020, offering insights into how channel market share responds to pricing and volume strategies.

#### Cost of Distribution

Every booking carries a cost. Managing these costs strategically is the single most important financial discipline in hotel commercial strategy.

**Direct channel costs** include website and app development, booking engine fees, payment processing (typically 2–3%), and digital marketing spend (SEM, social, email) to drive traffic. These costs are real but largely *fixed or controllable* — the hotel decides how much to invest.

**Indirect channel costs** are more visible and often *variable*:
- OTA commissions: 12–30% of the booking value
- GDS transaction fees: $5–$12 per reservation plus travel agent commissions
- Wholesale margins: the gap between the net rate and the eventual selling price (often 20–30%)

The metric that ties it all together is **Net RevPAR** — revenue per available room *after* deducting distribution costs. As Chapter 3 demonstrates through detailed worked examples, two hotels with identical gross RevPAR can have dramatically different profitability depending on their channel mix.

#### The Dynamic Ecosystem

The distribution landscape is not a static diagram — it is a living ecosystem where players constantly compete, collaborate, and shift strategies. Hotels and OTAs exist in what industry veterans often call a "frenemy" relationship: OTAs deliver guests the hotel could not reach on its own, but they also commoditize the product, own the customer data, and extract significant margin.

The balance of power never holds still. The HSMAI Foundation's 2012 analysis presciently warned of emerging "gatekeepers" like Google and Apple inserting themselves into the distribution chain (Estis Green & Lomanno, 2012, p. 2). That prediction has been realized: Google now influences hotel bookings at virtually every stage of the traveler journey — from the initial search, through Google Hotel Ads and Maps, to Google Travel and AI-powered trip planning.

**The AI disruption begins.** Generative AI platforms like ChatGPT and Google's Gemini are changing how travelers discover and book hotels, potentially disrupting the intermediary model that has dominated for two decades (Zanpure, 2025). The threat is significant enough that both Marriott and Hilton flagged AI platforms as material risks in their 2026 SEC filings (O'Neill, 2026). Chapter 13 examines this emerging challenge comprehensively.

**The regulatory landscape is shifting, too.** As distribution becomes increasingly digital, the industry faces a complex and expanding patchwork of regulations. New U.S. state-level data privacy laws effective in 2025–2026 complicate consent, data sharing, and targeted advertising practices. GDPR's rules on profiling and automated decision-making are becoming central to how traveler data can power AI-driven personalization and dynamic pricing. And scrutiny of "junk fees" and all-in pricing has resulted in state-level enforcement actions against travel platforms (Deloitte, 2026). For distribution strategists, regulatory compliance is no longer a back-office concern — it shapes which personalization tactics are permissible, how pricing can be displayed, and what data can flow between systems.


## 1.5 Why This Matters for Your Career

Distribution knowledge is no longer a specialization — it is a core business competency that touches every corner of hotel management:

- **General Managers & Operations Leaders:** You'll make daily decisions influenced by which channels are filling your hotel, at what rates, and at what cost. Understanding distribution means understanding your P&L.

- **Revenue Management Professionals:** Channel analysis is the bedrock of pricing, inventory allocation, and forecasting. You cannot optimize what you do not understand.

- **Sales & Marketing Executives:** Every campaign you run, every brand partnership you forge, every loyalty promotion you design exists within the distribution ecosystem. Knowing how channels interact prevents costly mistakes like inadvertently cannibalizing your own direct business.

- **Hotel Owners & Asset Managers:** Distribution cost is often the single largest variable expense after labor. A 3-percentage-point shift in channel mix from OTA to direct can translate to hundreds of thousands of dollars in additional profit annually for a single property.

- **Hospitality Technologists:** The entire ecosystem runs on technology — PMS, CRS, channel managers, RMS, CRM, payment gateways. Innovating in this space is one of the fastest-growing career paths in the industry.

Intense competition, hyper-informed consumers, and relentless technological change define this industry. The ability to think strategically about distribution — to leverage data, adapt to new channels, and manage complex partner relationships — separates competent operators from exceptional ones.


## Industry Spotlight: Booking Holdings vs. Marriott International — Two Titans, Two Models

To understand the tension at the heart of hotel distribution, compare two of the industry's most powerful companies:

**Booking Holdings** (2024 gross travel bookings: **$166 billion**; market capitalization: **$186 billion** as of mid-2025) operates the world's dominant OTA portfolio — Booking.com, Priceline, Agoda, and Kayak. Its business model is pure intermediation: it owns no hotels but commands enormous consumer traffic and charges hotels 12–30% commission for every booking it delivers. The decade from 2008 to 2019 was a golden era for the OTA duopoly: Booking Holdings and Expedia Group collectively grew gross bookings at a **20% compound annual growth rate** from 2003 to 2019 and expanded their combined share of the global hotel market from roughly **5%** in 2010 to **22%** by 2019. Within the online hotel market alone, the two companies doubled their share from **20% to 48%** over the same period (Agarwal, 2024a). That growth trajectory has since decelerated — both companies now post single-digit annual growth, roughly in line with the major hotel groups (Agarwal, 2025b).

**Marriott International** (2024: ~9,000 properties, 1.7 billion sq. ft. of meeting space, and over 210 million Bonvoy loyalty members) represents the opposite approach — a brand-led model where the hotel company invests billions in loyalty programs, brand.com technology, and marketing to drive guests into direct channels where margins are highest.

The two models are locked in a perpetual strategic chess match. When Marriott launched its "Member Rate" initiative — offering loyalty members a guaranteed discount on Marriott.com — Booking.com responded by investing in its own loyalty program (Genius) and expanding into alternative accommodations. When Booking.com pushed into the payments space to own more of the transaction, Marriott doubled down on its app experience.

**The lesson for students:** Distribution strategy is not just about choosing channels — it is about understanding the *power dynamics* between hotels and intermediaries, and making deliberate choices about where to invest, where to partner, and where to compete.


## Chapter Summary

This chapter has laid the foundation for your exploration of hotel distribution:

- **Distribution is the commercial engine** of the hospitality industry, connecting hotel supply to global demand through an intricate network of channels.
- **The ecosystem evolved** through five distinct phases — from handwritten ledgers to GDS networks to the internet revolution to today's mobile, social, AI-influenced landscape.
- **Today's channel landscape** includes direct channels (brand.com, voice, walk-in, group) and indirect channels (OTAs, GDS, wholesalers, metasearch), each with distinct economics and strategic implications.
- **Channel mix and cost of distribution** are the foundational concepts that drive profitability — and every decision you make in this industry will be shaped by them.
- **The ecosystem is dynamic** — the balance of power between hotels, intermediaries, and technology platforms shifts constantly, creating both risk and opportunity.

Distribution is not an academic exercise — it is the operating system of a successful hospitality career.
